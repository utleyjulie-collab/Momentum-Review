Phantasma's Guide to Momentum, Impulse, & Collisions

Alright folks, settle in. Let's talk about momentum, impulse, and collisions.

This is, without a doubt, one of the most important topics in all of physics. It shows up everywhere, from Paper 1 to Paper 2, and (if you're clever) it can even be the foundation of a great IA. From my engineering days, I can tell you this is what governs... well, everything that moves and hits something else.

So, let's get this straight. Here's your review guide. Don't just read it; use it.

1. Momentum (p)

  • What it is: Think of it as "inertia in motion." It’s a measure of how much "oomph" a moving object has and, consequently, how hard it is to stop.
  • The Gotcha: Momentum is a VECTOR. It has magnitude and direction. This is the #1 place students lose points. Don't be that student.
  • The Equation: 
    p = mv
    • p: momentum (in kg⋅m/s)
    • m: mass (in kg)
    • v: velocity (in m/s)
  • Because it's a vector: If a 10 kg ball moves right at 5 m/s, its momentum is +50 kg⋅m/s. If an identical ball moves left at 5 m/s, its momentum is -50 kg⋅m/s.

2. Impulse (J)

  • What it is: Impulse is the change in momentum (Δp). To change an object's momentum (e.g., stop it, speed it up, turn it), you have to apply a force over a period of time. That "force-over-time" is the impulse.
  • The Equations (Two sides of the same coin):
    1. Impulse as the cause: J = F_avg ⋅ Δt
    2. Impulse as the effect: J = Δp = p_final - p_initial
  • The Big Link (Impulse-Momentum Theorem): This is just putting the two equations together. It's incredibly powerful. 
    F_avg ⋅ Δt = Δp
  • Real-World Example: Why do you "follow through" in golf or baseball? To keep the force (F) acting on the ball for the longest possible time (Δt). A bigger (F⋅Δt) gives a bigger impulse (J), which means a bigger change in momentum (Δp), and thus a much higher final velocity for the ball.
  • Graphs: If you see a Force vs. Time graph, the area under the curve is the impulse. This is a classic exam question.

3. The Big Boss: Conservation of Linear Momentum

This is the law. This is the one you'll use over and over.

  • The Law: In an isolated system (meaning no external net forces like friction or air resistance are messing with it), the total momentum of the system before an interaction is equal to the total momentum of the system after the interaction.
  • The "Magic" Equation: 
    Σp_initial = Σp_final
  • For two objects colliding (A and B): 
    p_A(initial) + p_B(initial) = p_A(final) + p_B(final)

m_A⋅u_A + m_B⋅u_B = m_A⋅v_A + m_B⋅v_B
    (We often use 'u' for initial velocity and 'v' for final velocity. Get used to it.)

4. Applications: Collisions and Explosions

This is where you apply the conservation law. This breaks down into two types: simple 1-dimensional problems and more complex 2-dimensional problems.

A. 1D Collisions and Explosions

This is when everything happens along a single straight line (e.g., "forward/back" or "left/right").

Inelastic Collisions:

  • Momentum is... CONSERVED.
  • Kinetic Energy is... NOT conserved (it's lost to heat, sound, or deforming the objects).
  • Example: Two blobs of clay hit and stick. A car crash.
  • Perfectly Inelastic: This is the most extreme case where the objects stick together after colliding and move with a single, shared final velocity.

Elastic Collisions:

  • Momentum is... CONSERVED.
  • Kinetic Energy is... ALSO CONSERVED.
  • Example: Billiard balls (it's very close). Ideal gas particles.
  • Pro-Tip: If a problem states the collision is "elastic," it's a huge gift. It means you get two conservation equations to solve for your two unknowns (usually the two final velocities):
    1. Σp_initial = Σp_final
    2. ΣKE_initial = ΣKE_final

Explosions:

  • This is just a collision in reverse. Things start together (often at rest) and fly apart.
  • Example: A cannon firing a cannonball.
    • Before: The system (cannon + ball) is at rest. p_total(initial) = 0.
    • After: The ball moves forward (+p_ball) and the cannon recoils backward (–p_cannon).
    • By conservation: p_total(final) = 0, which means (+p_ball) + (–p_cannon) = 0.
    • This tells you that p_ball = p_cannon. The ball and cannon have equal and opposite momenta. (The ball is fast with low mass; the cannon is slow with high mass).

B. 2D Collisions (The Component Method)

This is where you really get to show you know what you're doing. The problem is that "oomph" (momentum) doesn't just go forward and back; it can also go sideways.

The Core Idea

The most wonderful, beautiful, and (if you're an engineer, essential) truth is this: Physics in the x-direction is completely independent of physics in the y-direction.

This means our one "magic" conservation equation, Σp_initial = Σp_final, is actually two separate, simpler equations:

  1. X-Direction: Σp_x(initial) = Σp_x(final)
  2. Y-Direction: Σp_y(initial) = Σp_y(final)

Your Step-by-Step Guide to Not Getting Lost

Follow this process. Every single time.

  1. Draw and Label (The "Before" and "After")
    • Draw your coordinate system (an x-y axis). Pro-Tip: Be smart. If one object is moving perfectly horizontally, make that your x-axis. It'll save you a ton of math.
    • Draw all your objects before they hit. Label their masses (`m_A`, `m_B`) and their initial velocities (`u_A`, `u_B`).
    • Draw all your objects after they hit. Label their final velocities (`v_A`, `v_B`).
    • Crucially: Label any and all angles. These angles must be measured from one of your axes.
  2. Resolve ALL Velocities into Components

    This is the step everyone messes up. Any velocity vector v at an angle θ (relative to the x-axis) must be broken down:

    v_x = v ⋅ cos(θ)
v_y = v ⋅ sin(θ)

    Do this for every single velocity (`u_A`, `u_B`, `v_A`, `v_B`). Create a table if it helps. Be careful with your signs (e.g., moving left is a negative x-component, moving down is a negative y-component).

  3. Write Your Two Momentum Equations

    X-Equation:

    m_A⋅u_Ax + m_B⋅u_Bx = m_A⋅v_Ax + m_B⋅v_Bx

    Y-Equation:

    m_A⋅u_Ay + m_B⋅u_By = m_A⋅v_Ay + m_B⋅v_By
  4. Solve the System

    You now have a system of two algebraic equations. In a typical exam problem, you'll have two unknowns (like the final speed v_A and the final angle θ_A). This is now just a (sometimes messy) math problem.

What About Kinetic Energy in 2D?

  • Kinetic Energy (KE) is a SCALAR. It has no components. It's just a number. It has no direction.
  • You never resolve KE into x and y components. That's nonsense.
  • You use the full speed* (magnitude) of the velocity: KE = ½mv².
  • To find the full speed from components, use Pythagoras: v² = v_x² + v_y².
  • If the collision is ELASTIC: You get a third equation to help you solve: 
    ΣKE_initial = ΣKE_final
½m_A(u_A)² + ½m_B(u_B)² = ½m_A(v_A)² + ½m_B(v_B)²
  • If the collision is INELASTIC: KE is *not* conserved. You *cannot* use the equation above. Instead, a question will often ask *how much* KE was lost. You just calculate KE_total(initial) and KE_total(final) separately and find the difference.

5. The "Real" Newton's Second Law

  • What you learned: F_net = ma
  • What's really true: Net Force is the rate of change of momentum.
  • The Equation: 
    F_net = Δp / Δt
  • Hold on, did I lie to you? Not really. Look:
    • If F_net = Δp / Δt and p = mv, then F_net = Δ(mv) / Δt.
    • For most of your problems, mass is constant. So you can pull it out: F_net = m ⋅ (Δv / Δt).
    • And what is Δv / Δt? It's acceleration, a.
    • So... F_net = ma.
  • Why it matters: The IB syllabus specifically notes that F_net = ma assumes mass is constant. The F_net = Δp / Δt form is the true definition and works even when mass is changing (like a rocket burning fuel).

6. Phantasma's Pro-Tips for Not Messing This Up

  1. VECTORS! Did I say this? Draw a diagram. Pick a direction as "positive" (e.g., to the right, and up). Any velocity going left or down must be a negative number in your equation.
  2. ISOLATE THE SYSTEM. Be clear about what's inside your system before and after. Momentum is conserved for the system, not necessarily for one object in it.
  3. MOMENTUM vs. KINETIC ENERGY. They are NOT the same.
    • Momentum (p = mv) is conserved in all isolated collisions (1D and 2D).
    • Kinetic Energy (KE = ½mv²) is only conserved in elastic collisions.
  4. CHECK THE QUESTION. Does it say "elastic"? "Inelastic"? "Sticks together"? These words are clues that tell you what to do with Kinetic Energy.
  5. 2D IS JUST TWO 1D PROBLEMS. Don't panic when you see angles. Just turn it into one "x-problem" and one "y-problem" and solve them. Be meticulous.

That's it. Go do some practice problems. Seriously. Go on.
